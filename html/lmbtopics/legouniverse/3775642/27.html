Cool!

 

One of the best ways to make the main character interesting is to make them human. Wait; this is a LU story, "human" is a bad way to put it. Make them fallible. (Did I spell that right?) They have to have faults. Or else no one can relate to them or sympathize.

 

Also, backstory is EXTREMELY IMPORTANT!!! Of course, the book can be their backstory and show how they became whatever they become at the end, but if you're putting them in the story when they are in the middle of their life, then it helps to have a (usually dark) past. Depending on the character their past doesn't have to be dark, but it can help for the development. I think that is the most important piece to a character: Development. Showing how they grow, or not grow, gives the reader an insight and thus interest.

*Looks up* I wasn't leaving, I was just turning my attention to this bag of potato chips. Er, what was a bag of potato chips. *Wipes salt off cheek* Now it's just a bag.

 

Nevermind that. Here's some more tips.

 

Scratch what I said about development being the most imporant thing. That should be second. Emotion is the most important thing that covers just about everything you need for a character. Without emotion your character isn't human; has no development; nothing. However! Some characters are built upon no emotion, which can be great. For instance, having a character with no emotion going through a (normally) heart-rending crisis can make for a good story. It depends on your character's personality.

 

To sum it up, emotion is what makes a character believable. It makes them real. Imagine a world without emotion- go ahead, imagine it- scary, huh?

 

Oh, and to clear something up; emotion doesn't have to be romantic!! While yes, romance can be good for some stories, it's not necessary. When I say emotion I mean angry, estatic, offended, etc.

 

On another note: IF YOU ARE GOING TO SMASH A CHARACTER IN YOUR STORY, () you need to leave a VOID in their place. A utterly empty place in the universe- you must make the reader feel like the world is no longer complete without this fictional person. I've given this a lot of thought actually. (I've been writing a story where something like this happens) It's difficult to make a reader become attatched to a character for who they are, although not impossible. An easier way is to make the OTHER characters become attatched to the doomed character. (Again, romance is not necessary) When the character dies, the reader will feel sympathy for the survivers' loss and thus endear them to the remaining characters.
