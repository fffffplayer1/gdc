Attention, members of the Kingdom and allies! After the phenomenal test runs of the Gamma Project, which will be deployed to all fleets immediately, it is time for a new weapon. The Gamma Project will allow us to capture ships undamaged and slaughter large groups of hostile forces at will, but now we need an anti-cruiser weapon that can hit what our other weapons can't. What I have in mind is a unique missle, completely undetectable to the naked eye and very hard to detect on scanner in battle. This missle, if my theories are accurate, could even phase through shields and armor alike as if they didn't exist, then detonate with a blast equal to or greater than the force of a starship's fusion reactor.
JAMMERS ON!!! GA ONLY!!!

Sorry, can't let the DRE see this. Anyway, what I was avoiding detailing was that, if we could enclose a charge of antimatter within a sleeve of dark matter, we could have our superweapon. The idea is that dark matter doesn't interact with matter normally, and is only affected by specially designed magnets and gravity. If a system for telling the missle to detonate could be devised ( Personally, I think it may be best to let the magnetism of an enemy ship's reactors do the job, although there are other possibilites.), then, once the drak matter sleeve has gotten the natimatter past the scanners, shields, and hull of a craft, we can surprise an enemy with a massive antimatter blast that should do severe, if not fatal, damage to the enemy. This project's need for secrecy can't be overstated; if the DRE beat us to the punch on building this weapon, they could rip us to shreds in a matter of weeks, if not days. When it is built, the first targets should be the Vanguard, all DRE research facilities, and any other flagships. This will render them crippled, and ready for combat with our fleet. Also, by the way, the Gamma Project was an effort to build a Gamma Ray Pulse Cannon. Gamma rays are so lethal that a burst with several thousand light years from the right object could obliterate Earthborne life very quickly. This weapon is nowehere near as powerful for obvious reasons, and it is ineffective when the enemy has energy shields up, but nothing else we tested could block a hit.The idea is that, if the shields are gone, then a sngle pulse can end the crew and allow us to capture the ship.
JAMMERS OFF!!!!!

* Dozens of technicians begin to fiddle with gravity meters, particle accelerators, antimatter weapon storage units, and very large magnets.*

It is only a little while longer, my friends. Just hold out a few more weeks, and we will be able to push the Empire back.
-Bane

All the info you want on Gamma Rays:

"Gamma radiation, also known as gamma rays or hyphenated as gamma-rays and denoted as γ, is electromagnetic radiation of high frequency and therefore energy. Gamma rays are ionizing radiation and are thus biologically hazardous. Gamma rays are classically produced by the decay from high energy states of atomic nuclei (gamma decay), but also in many other ways. Natural sources of gamma rays on Earth include gamma decay from naturally-occurring radioisotopes such as potassium-40, and also as a secondary radiation from various atmospheric interactions with cosmic ray particles. Some rare terrestrial natural sources that produce gamma rays that are not of a nuclear origin, are lightning strikes and terrestrial gamma-ray flashes, which produce high energy emissions from natural high-energy voltages. Gamma rays are produced by a number of astronomical processes in which very high-energy electrons are produced. Such electrons produce secondary gamma rays by the mechanisms of bremsstrahlung, inverse Compton scattering and synchrotron radiation. A large fraction of such astronomical gamma rays are screened by Earth's atmosphere and must be detected by spacecraft. Notable artificial sources of gamma rays include fission such as occurs in nuclear reactors, and high energy physics experiments, such as neutral pion decay and nuclear fusion.

Shielding from gamma rays requires large amounts of mass, in contrast to alpha particles which can be blocked by paper or skin, and beta particles which can be shielded by foil. Gamma rays are better absorbed by materials with high atomic numbers and high density, although neither effect is important compared to the total mass per area in the path of the gamma ray. For this reason, a lead shield is only modestly better (20–30% better) as a gamma shield, than an equal mass of another shielding material such as aluminium, concrete, water or soil; lead's major advantage is not in lower weight, but rather its compactness due to its higher density. Protective clothing, goggles and respirators can protect from internal contact with or ingestion of alpha or beta particles, but provide no protection from gamma radiation from external sources.
The higher the energy of the gamma rays, the thicker the shielding required. Materials for shielding gamma rays are typically measured by the thickness required to reduce the intensity of the gamma rays by one half (the half value layer or HVL). For example gamma rays that require 1 cm (0.4″) of lead to reduce their intensity by 50% will also have their intensity reduced in half by 4.1 cm of granite rock, 6 cm (2½″) of concrete, or 9 cm (3½″) of packed soil. However, the mass of this much concrete or soil is only 20–30% greater than that of lead with the same absorption capability. Depleted uranium is used for shielding in portable gamma ray sources, but again the savings in weight over lead is modest, and the main effect is to reduce shielding bulk. In a nuclear power plant, shielding can be provided by steel and concrete in the pressure and particle containment vessel, while water provides a radiation shielding of fuel rods during storage or transport into the reactor core. The loss of water or removal of a "hot" fuel assembly into the air would result in much higher radiation levels than when kept under water."
