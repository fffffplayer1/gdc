‎07-28-2014 04:24 PM

“What happened?!” he asked urgently.

 

“I don’t know,” Axolc confessed.  “We’ve lost our left rotor, but I can’t figure out how or why-”  As he worked with the machine, his gaze fell onto a flashing green display in front of him, and he stared at it for a minute.  “Oh,” was all he could muster.

 

“What?!!” the Major cried impatiently.

 

“The primary sensors array is out, but our auxiliary sensors have picked up a large airborne vessel less than 30 kilometers behind us.”  He flicked a switch on the board in front of him, then two more on the ceiling above him, and the primary lights in the cockpit went out, replaced by dim red ones, indicating he had initiated the ship’s pre-combat protocols.  “Who wants to guess they’re hostile?”

 

The Major hit a switch on his side of the ceiling, and heard the sound of the cockpit doors sealing shut, followed by an alert to the passenger bay for all hands to strap into their seats and/or assume combat stations.

 

“What are we up against?” the Major asked, as he ran a schematics check on the left wing.

 

“I have no idea!” Axolc said, gritting his teeth together.  “Without our primary sensors, I can’t tell how big, how fast, or even exactly how far away they are.  And I certainly can’t run a scan of their weapons systems.”

‎07-28-2014 04:27 PM

The Major keyed open his comlink. “Praxer!” he shouted into it.

 

“Yes sir?”

 

“I need you to hop into one of the turrets and get me a visual.  Let us know exactly what we’re up against.”

 

“Yes sir!”

 

A few seconds later, the ships readouts picked up the right dorsal beam turret as currently occupied, and Praxer’s voice came through the com slightly fragmented: “Ar--y---seeing---is--ajor?”

 

“Didn’t read that, say again?”

 

“---UGE----must be----ster”

 

“Praxer, you do realize that the turret has its own comlink, right?”

 

There was a pause, and then Praxer’s voice came clear over the intercom.  “Sorry, it’s been a while since I used one of these.  Anyways, whoever our pursuers are, they aren’t kidding around!  That thing is four times the size of Nimbus Plaza!!”

 

THAT was a frightening thought.  “What does it look like, other that its size?”

 

“It’s got half a dozen heavy dual-laser canons, I’d say about Earthshaker-Class at the very least, and it’s covered from head to toe in smaller weaponry.  I’ve never seen so much firepower in one place!”

 

“I have…” the Major said, more to himself than to Praxer.  “Think we can outrun it?”

 

“At the rate it’s moving, we have only about three minutes.  That is, assuming they’re interested in taking us alive and aren’t just waiting for those laser canons to cool down.”

 

The Major cursed.  This was not good.  He looked around the cockpit at the ship’s readout.  About half their systems were dead or dying, and with the left wing inoperable, they were sitting ducks.  If they didn’t find a way to get the wing’s rotor back online, their pursuers might not even need to take a second shot.

‎07-28-2014 04:28 PM

“How’s the left wing look from your end?” he called out to Praxer.  

 

“It’s a bit outside my range of sight, but I’ll have a look,” Praxer replied.  As the Major watched his schematics, the light indicating that the right dorsal turret was occupied switched off, and the one for the belly turret switched on.  A minute later, Praxer answered.

 

“Sir, the left rotor is gone,” Praxer announced.  “What’s more, it looks like they’re charging up those laser canons…”

 

“Quick!” the Major called to Axolc.  “Bring us around so that they have a clear shot of our right wing.  But make it look natural.”

 

Axolc looked at him with bafflement written all over his face, but he did as the Major instructed, and turned the ship 85 degrees to the right and held her there.  A few seconds later, a familiar shudder ran through the ship, and the readout for the right wing blinked out.

 

“Well, there you go!  One right wing demolished!  Now if you have any other brilliant ideas…?”

 

“Swing us back around,” the Major instructed, ignoring Axolc’s jab.  “But go the long way.  I want to see these people for myself.”

‎07-28-2014 04:28 PM

Axolc muttered something inaudible, and turned the ship hard to the right.  As the vision in the windshield panned, the Major scrutinized the skies, though when he saw the pursuing ship, he realized that it would be impossible to miss the approaching cruiser anyways.

 

It was MASSIVE.

 

Praxer had not exaggerated the size of the ship; if anything, he had underestimated it.  The thing was HUGE!!!  The Major was in so much shock that he almost forgot the rest of his plan.  He observed the giant airborne battleship, and noted that the heavy artillery weapons Praxer had described were all on the dorsal side of the ship.  Perfect.

 

“Let them get closer,” the Major instructed.  “When they reach the 10-kilometer mark, activate our auxiliary engines.  We’re going to skim the underside of this monster and escape before they know what happened. 

 

“Better make it 5-kilometers then,” Axolc muttered.  “I don’t know how far the auxiliary power will be able to carry us.  Also, I hate to bring it up, but we’re losing altitude.  At current we have nothing keeping us in the air.  Also, I think our friends know it too, because they’ve stopped advancing on us.”

 

The Major looked down at his projections, and realized he was right.  They were starting to plummet to the surface, and at the rate they were falling, there was no reason for their pursuers to bother trying to catch them; as far as their pursuer’s were concerned, they had been successfully shot down, and all that was left was to wait until they hit ground zero and then pick up the pieces.  So much for THAT idea.

‎07-28-2014 04:30 PM

“Okay, you make the calls,” the Major said, unstrapping himself from his seat.  “Try to get us over water; I’m going to go see what I can do to boost the auxiliary power.”

 

“What water?!  We’re flying over a desert!”

 

“The river that feeds into Lake Nimbia is only a few kilometers to the south,” the Major recited from memory, hoping desperately that his recollection of their map was right.  “Just hang in there and I’ll try to give us an opening.”

 

With that, he opened the door to the passenger bay, turning on his comlink as he did so.  “Praxer, I need you to keep your eyes peeled below,” he said as he sealed the door shut behind him.  “When the desert floor is in range, fire every bit of that turret’s energy into it.  See if you can kick up a storm; it’ll help cover our escape.  I’m assuming there are no clouds obscuring your vision?”

 

“It’s a desert.  What do you think?”

 

“Good man.”

 

He keyed off his comlink just as he reached the far end of the passenger bay.  Reyna and Jonathon were strapped in, side-by-side, in the row next to him.  Jonathon looked a bit distraught, but Reyna seemed more annoyed than worried.

 

“I hate this!” she huffed.  “Being cooped up in here while everyone else scurries around trying to save our hides is NOT my idea of a good time!”

 

“If you want to be helpful, you can get into one of the turrets and help Praxer,” the Major asked, punching the access code for the cargo and maintenance section into the cargo bay hatch.  “Or see if you can get our primary sensors back online.  But don’t just sit there complaining, because there’s plenty of stuff for you to do.”  He turned to face them.  “Jonathon, if you want to help, you can come with me.”

Chapter 79 - Part 10

‎07-28-2014 04:32 PM

Jonathon nodded, and unbuckled himself.  Reyna had already done likewise, and was already across the room, fiddling around with a jungle of wires behind an open panel in the wall.  The Major keyed in the final digit of the access code and heard the click of the hatch unlocking.  When no further response came, he forced it open with a grunt.  He then stormed inside, with Jonathon scurrying along behind him.

 

They entered into a room lined with electronics and pipes and valves, the inner workings of the dropship.  The Major wasted no time in finding the auxiliary power control unit.  He unlatched the cover, and flipped the board open with an ominous hiss.  The tangled mess of wires facing him was daunting, but not incomprehensible. 

 

“We need to boost the power,” the Major said.  “See if you can find the power lines running to the wings.  With any luck, the main power supply will have received minimal damage, and we may be able to reroute it to here.”

 

Jonathon nodded, and started sliding down the walls, scanning the mechanical mess for what he was looking for.  The Major, meanwhile, began searching for the secondary coolant valves; according to the auxiliary systems display, one of the four coolant lines was no longer bringing coolant down to the main systems, which could have disastrous side-effects.  If the pump had stopped working or the pipes had sprung a leak, they would all burn up before they ever reached the ground if it wasn’t patched up.

‎07-28-2014 04:33 PM

He followed the coolant lines down until he found the broken valve.  It was nothing catastrophically bad: one of the blasts had knocked the valve loose, so the control mechanism had shut down the pumping mechanism and sealed off the supply to prevent leakage.  Fixing it was as simple as tightening the loose connection and reactivating the pump.  This done, the Major returned to the auxiliary power control panel, where he found Jonathon already waiting for him.

 

“Did you find it?” the Major asked, not wasting a breath.

 

Jonathon held up a neat bundle of several wires.  Their ends were all shaved off, and sparks relentlessly shot forth from their exposed tips, but Jonathon’s hands were far enough below the ends to avoid getting zapped.  The ends that weren’t visible dragged behind him and into some unknown portion of the ship.

 

“Good man,” Major said.  “Now let’s find a place to link those up.”

 

But no sooner had the words left his mouth, when another shudder ran through the ship, and knocked the Major straight off his feet.  The last thing he remembered was the sickening thud of his forehead slamming against something hard.
