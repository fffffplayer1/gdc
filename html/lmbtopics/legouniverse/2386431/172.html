‎01-18-2014 12:04 PM

Instead of answering the question, however, Reyna seized her chance and grabbed the power wand, but instead of letting go in surprise as she had expected, the Jester held fast, and maintained his grip on the wand.  Worse, Reyna had not held it for two seconds before she felt a powerful surge of electricity come up through the wand and electrocute her, forcing her to release her grip and fall down to the ground in pain.  As she wheezed, however, she made note of the color of each of her interrogator’s wands, and also noted that their hats were not matted leather, but rather and indescribably fabric that changed color like the mood rings she had seen at dollar stores.  The one who had shocked her wore Yellow, the one opposite him wore Red, and the one in the middle wore Light Blue.

 

“If you need further demonstration,” Light Blue said, not finishing his sentence in words, but instead calmly drawing his own wand and using it on the one who had shocked her, without breaking his gaze.  As he realized what was happening, Yellow screamed in horror, but was only able to make any noise for the fraction of a second before the ice coming from Light Blue’s wand completely enveloped him.  Still staring at Reyna, Light Blue put his wand back on his belt, and gave the frozen Jester a light one-handed push.  The ice mass fell over, and shattered into a million tiny fragments when it hit the ground, with Yellow still inside.

 

“Now, why were you on our network?” Light Blue resumed like nothing had happened.  Reyna was on the verge of tears.  She knew it was because she grabbed the wand that Light Blue had made an example of Yellow, and she felt an immense wave of guilt wash over her.

 

“You… monster…” she whimpered.

 

“My name is General Doavk,” Light Blue said.  “Why were you on our network?”

 

“You… YOU…”

“Jog her memory,” Droavk said, still staring unblinking at Reyna.  At this command, the remaining Jester, the one Reyna had named Red, drew his power wand and sent a whirl of flames into the cell.  Since she was still on the ground, and having no time to react, Reyna was caught on full force, and screamed in agony.  The flames stung eyes and face, causing immeasurable pain.

 

“Enough,” Droavk said, and the flames stopped.  “She has nothing to say.  We will see if her friends are any different.  If not, we will begin the protocol for formal interrogation.”

 

Reyna wanted to scream in rage at the fiends, but all she could manage was a soft whisper.  She felt so helpless, and completely out of her element.  As her tormentors turned to leave, she managed to crawl forward and moan, “wait…”

 

Silently, the General and his lackey turned around and looked at her with blank, emotionless faces.  Reyna, using the bars of her cell to hold herself up, took a deep breath…

 

And spat in the general’s face. 

 

Droavk wiped the insult off his cheek, and turned back around and left.  As Reyna realized he didn’t care, she finally gave up and fell into a deep sleep.

When she woke up again, she found herself lying on the concrete bed in her cell, with a Jester standing over her.  She wanted to reach up and break his neck, but she was so weak that she couldn’t lift her arm half an inch before she let it fall back down in exhaustion.  And, after realizing that the Jester was actually a medic who was in her cell to treat her burns, she decided he was probably best left as he was.

 

“The next time I see that miserable pile of bricks…” Reyna muttered to herself. 

 

The doctor heard her though.  “If you mean Droavk, I wouldn’t try it,” he admonished.  “Even if you ever did get out of this cell.”

 

“Oh don’t worry, I’ve already planned out my escape,” Reyna lied.

 

“Really?” the doctor replied, not believing a word of it.  “And how will you do that?”

 

“Uhh… If I told you, I’d have to smash you,” Reyna countered.

 

“Right…” the doctor said with a smile, and went back to treating her wounds. 

 

Reyna turned away and went back to staring up at the ceiling.  Eventually her eyelids began to feel heavy, and she decided to let them rest for a few minutes.  When she woke up, the doctor was gone, and she had no idea how much time had passed.

‎01-18-2014 12:07 PM

She sat up with a groan, and rubbed her forehead.  She appeared to be alone, so she took the chance to observe her surroundings.  Her cell looked into a circular room that contained about half a dozen more, with a single, bolted door connecting it to the outside world.  A ring of keys hung on a hook next to the door, though for some reason there appeared to be far more keys than there were cells.  In the center of the room was a simple wooden table, upon which her armor and weapons had been laid to rest.  Wow, these guys must not have prisoners very often, or they would never be so careless!

 

Suddenly the door opened, and two Jesters walked in.  The first one carried a tray in his hands, the other one held a spear tipped with the same star-top as all the Jester power wands Reyna had seen.  The two walked over to her cell in single file, and as the one in the back pushed a button in the wall, a slit in the cell bars opened up on the floor, through which the first one slid his tray.  Then the slit closed and the Jester in front turned around and left the room again, leaving the other one standing guard. 

 

Reyna looked down at the dinner she had been given.  It consisted of a single loaf of bread and a glass of what must have once been water.  It was crude, but at least she wouldn’t starve. 

 

With a shrug, she brought the bread up to her mouth for a taste.  But when she bit down on it, she found it was as hard as rock, and that no matter how hard she tried her mandibles couldn’t put so much as a dent in it.  The next thing she realized was that she had been holding her breath, and when she inhaled again after letting it go, she caught her first whiff of the bread’s stench.  Disgusted, she put the bread back on the tray.  Whether or not it actually was edible or not was no longer a risk she was willing to take.

She sighed, and sat down on her bed, wishing it was more than just a raised platform of the same concrete that formed the floor and walls.  She looked back out of her cell.  The guard was still there.  It seemed they didn’t trust her to be left to her own devices unwatched.  However, this also meant that they had left a weak link in the chain that bound her: a human link.  Now how to take advantage of it…

 

“Excuse me,” she called to the guard, “May I put in a request for more comfortable lodgings?”

 

“Uhh… no…” said the guard, confused. 

 

“Can I trade in my food?  I would actually prefer Chinese.”

 

“Uhh… not my business,” replied the guard, shaking his head. 

 

“What’s your name?”

 

“Dwayne.”

 

“And why do they call you that?”

 

“Uhh…”

 

Reyna sat back on her concrete slab.  Now she had a fairly good assessment of the guard, and the conclusion she reached was this: he was obviously rather slow, to put it mildly.  Now how to exploit this…

 

She looked around her cell for inspiration.  It was at times like these that concrete cells were always the worst; walls of stone or brick were usually more adept to give birth to ideas than solid, uniform ones.  Shrugging, Reyna looked out of her cell, when something caught her eye.

 

She looked down, and realized the guard had forgotten to close the food slit on the floor.  Hmmm…

She looked down at her loaf of bread, and inspiration struck.

 

“Hey!” she called out to the guard.  “Can I have a little help with this bread please?”

 

The guard stared at her, unsure of what to do.  Reyna hoped he wasn’t too stupid, or tricking him into doing what she wanted would probably be difficult.

 

“Just hand me one of my swords so I can cut it,” she said, with a serious demeanor and inflection to suggest what she was asking was just a reasonable request.

 

“Umm… I’m not sure about that…” the idiot guard said hesitantly. 

 

“Then… why don’t you take the bread and do it yourself?” Reyna suggested hopefully.  “Here.”

 

She held out the bread through the bars.  He hesitated a moment, then shrugged, seeing that there was probably no harm in doing this, and took the bread.  He put it down on the table and picked up one of Reyna’s katanas.  He then proceeded to make a valiant effort to cut the bread, but apparently even her katana was unable to so much as scratch it.  Shoot.

 

“You know… I don’t know if you should really be eating this…” the guard said out of the blue.  Reyna raised an eyebrow in a combination of surprise, amusement, and irritation.  Wow. 

 

“It doesn’t look very healthy…” the guard continued, and leaned in.  Before Reyna realized what he was doing, he took a small sniff of the bread, before she could stop him.  Apparently his olfactory sensors were more sensitive than Reyna’s, because the moment he did his eyes rolled back and he fainted, falling forward into the bread.  The bread did not cave in as normal bread would at such an impact, but remained rigid, and let out an audible ‘thud’ when it was hit, as if it were solid rock.  Reyna sighed; that did not go as planned.

Reyna ended up having to wait for the next guard rotation before anyone noticed that the current guard had been incapacitated.  His replacement, evidently smarter, though not by much, was incredulous towards her story, and shook the guard awake to confirm this.  He then sent the previous guard home and took up his post.

While she had been thinking this, the guard had pulled what looked like a small metal box out of his pocket, and had started staring at the open end, tapping the surface furiously.  From the distinct electronic sounds it was making as he did this, he must’ve been playing Galaga.

Then Reyna got an idea, way better than her first.  “Hey, can I have that bread back?” she asked.

 

“Hm?  Oh, sure.”  The guard walked over and handed her the bread through the bars, hardly looking up from his game before he had returned to the other side of the room.

 

Reyna took a deep breath.  The next step would only work if the guard remained absorbed in his game.  If he noticed what she was doing, it would be back to square one.

 

Slowly, gently, Reyna began rubbing the loaf of bread against the side of the rightmost bar in her cell.  After a few passes, she lifted her tool to inspect her work.  The difference was small, too small for an untrained eye to notice, but her eye was trained, and the bread had certainly left a tiny indent in the bar.

 

Reyna looked back at her supervisor.  He was still playing his game, unaware of anything she did that wasn’t directed at him.

 

So Reyna took another breath, and began slowly filing her way through the bars with the rock-hard loaf of bread they had actually tried to feed her.
