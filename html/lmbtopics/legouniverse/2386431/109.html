Chapter 59

 

With a slow grunt, the Major hauled himself over the battlements atop the wall and rolled over onto the walkway.  His gaze darted from side to side, making sure the guards were still away, before getting up and reaching down over the battlements.  A few seconds later he pulled Jonathon up and over the battlements, and soon the boy was crouched down next to him on the walkway.  With a nod, the Major led them down the walkway, crouching as low as possible and making sure to hug the side of the battlements.  It was as good as they could get to being invisible to anyone in the courtyard, so it would have to do.

 

In this manner, they made their way down the wall, away from the front gate and towards the keep.  The wall didn’t just turn a corner at the end, but was rather complimented by a tower raised well above the walkway, that connected the side and back walls.  They managed to reach this tower without incident.  So far, so good.

 

The Major stood up to open the wooden door that marked the entrance to the tower, but just before he grabbed the handle, the door swung open as if by itself.  On the other side was a lone Bat Warrior, looking as confused and startled as they were.  But then the warrior and the Major regained their composure at the exact same moment.

 

“GUAR-” the warrior’s cry of alarm was immediately cut off by the Major, who skipped introductions and simply swung his fist into the warrior’s face, knocking him out cold.  As the limp warrior started to topple backwards, the Major shot his grapple line and caught him just before he hit the ground, preventing him from causing anymore clamor.

With the body in hand, the Major cautiously stepped inside the small tower.  The room the door entered to was not so much a room as just a landing in the stairs, which spiraled down in front of them and then up again to their left.  The landing was relatively small, but still large enough to fit a decorative suit of armor standing in the corner between the stairs.  The Major did not see any decent options for what they actually needed: somewhere to hide the body of their new friend.  If this was a movie, they would do something ‘clever’ like hide the body inside the suit of armor.  In real life, they didn’t have time to get fancy, and trying to get a limp body inside a decorative suit of armor would take all night. 

 

With a sigh, the Major set the body of the guard on the ground and rolled him onto him back.  Cursing under his breath that they were wasting time they didn’t have, the Major positioned the warrior’s hands and head to make it look like he had just fallen asleep.  When he was done, he didn’t bother to admire his handiwork; good enough or not, it would have to do.

 

With that, he brushed off his hands and got back on his feet.  Signaling Jonathon to stay close, he crept over and looked through the porthole in the door to the back wall, where they were originally headed.  He abruptly backed away when he saw a guard coming towards them along the walkway, and beckoned Jonathon to run down the stairs.  The Major followed behind him, watching their backs.

When they had already made several rotations down the stairs, the Major heard the faint echo of the door being opened and a surprised grunt, indicating the guard had apparently noticed the last-minute job they did for the unconscious warrior.  The Major faintly heard an order, presumably to wake up, slowly repeated several times, growing louder with each successive attempt.  Eventually there was a muffled shout and proclamation, which probably meant the Major’s ploy had been discovered, as he had feared it would.  Now they would probably raise the alarm, and so he’d have to work under the assumption that they had. 

 

Sure enough, moments later there came the steady sound of approaching footsteps, as someone was evidently coming down the stairs to investigate.  The Major gestured to Jonathon to go faster, not wanting to see what would happen if they were caught, and they picked up the pace down the winding stairs.

 

After a few seconds they reached another landing, similar to the one they had seen moments ago.  A quick check of the doors confirmed that this one entered to the inside of the walls, and that the ground floor was still farther down.  The Major wanted to get as far away from the approaching footsteps as possible, but he also wanted to throw them of their trail.  Before continuing down the stairs, the Major opened one of the doors and left it ajar, hoping this would be enough to fool them.  Then he raced down the stairs after Jonathon with all speed. 

 

After a few seconds, the Major stopped stock still in his tracks, and put a hand on Jonathon’s shoulder to make sure he did the same.  The Major bent one ear upward, and waited.  After a moment, the steady sound of footsteps grew louder and stopped, presumably at the landing.  There was a pause for a moment, before the footsteps resumed at a faster pace, gradually fading out of earshot.  The Major gave a small sigh of relief; their pursuer had fallen for it, at least for the moment.

The Major did not waste any time gawking at this prospect however.  After taking a moment to regain his focus, the Major took the lead, as he and Jonathon resumed their descent and headed deeper into the Bat Lords’ Fortress.

 

***

 

When they reached the bottom of the stairs, the Major called Jonathon aside for a moment. 

 

“That was too close back there,” he whispered to the boy.  “If we’re going to be sneaking around in here, I think we should at least have some sort of escape plan in case of emergencies.”

 

The boy nodded, and so the Major led them both out of the shadows of the stairs and into the main room of the bottom of the tower.  It was much larger than the landings they had previously seen; in fact, it was almost big enough to actually be considered a room.  Still, it had the same basic layout as the levels they had previously seen, just on a slightly larger scale.  Besides that, the only real difference was the presence of a third door in between the two doors leading into the walls.  The door was set in the corner and angled between the other two doors at a perfect 45° angle from each.  The Major decided to try the new door and see where it led.

 

The third door, as it turned out, led to the courtyard outside.  The Major would have discarded this route and tried one of the other doors instantly, if they had been immediately exposed when they came through the door.  However, they had come out on a walkway underneath a thatched roof, with a wooden railing lined with pillars that held the roof up.  If they kept to the back wall, they would be much less obvious than in the open courtyard, and they would still get some of the benefits the courtyard gave, such as a full range of vision and a clear view of all the inner walls.

With a shrug, the Major led Jonathon down the walkway towards the gatehouse.  When they were halfway down the wall, the walkway stopped at a house-sized wooden structure built into the wall.  The door was set underneath the walkway, so, having no better options, the Major led the way in.

 

Inside it became clear what the building was: the stables.  Horse pens lined the walls in rows, and the horses were currently engaged in a loud series of chatter.  Good, that meant any noise they made would be drowned out.

 

The Major briskly strode down the rows of pens, examining the horses as he went.  If they could find a good pair of reliable horses, that would take care of half their escape plan.  It was an opportunity, and the Major intended to use it.  

 

The Major looked from horse to horse, examining each as he went.  He wasn’t really an expert on this sort of thing, so he had no idea which horses would be preferable, or even listen to him.

 

Eventually, he paused in front of a row of horses along the wall and whistled sharply. 

 

Most of the horses ignored him. 

 

Some turn their heads to look.

 

And two, just two, not only shot their heads in his direction, but tried to break out of their pens to come to him. 

 

The Major nodded, and prepped these two for a speedy departure.  He then reached into his bag and felt around until he found what he was looking for.

 

“Here,” he said to Jonathon, handing him his old Bat disguise.  “We’re going to leave these two horses outside the gatehouse in case we need them.  We’re going to wear these just in case.”

They trotted down the courtyard path ambling along towards the gatehouse.  When they reached their destination, they were promptly confronted by the gatekeeper. 

 

“Who are you, and what is your business?”

 

The Major took a deep breath, and responded, “We are warriors who would like nothing more than to be back in bed and fast asleep.  We are only here because High Lord Zanjor requested that we leave two horses by the gatehouse for his use in the near-future.”

 

“Use for what?  And why wasn’t I informed?”

 

The Major shrugged.  “Ask him yourself.  Who knows why commander creep does anything these days?  Like I said, I’d rather be in bed, but I’m just doing what he ordered me to do.”

 

The gatekeeper sighed.  “Very well… here, let me take them for you.”

 

“Much obliged sir.”

 

“Well, it’s the least we can do.  Zanjor’s been getting on all our backs lately.  Goodness knows what he’ll do next!”

 

“Thanks.  Say, can I step inside the gatehouse for a moment?  It’s kinda chilly out here.”

 

The gatekeeper paused for a moment, and then replied, “Sure, go on ahead.  The door’s right over here.”  And with that, he led them to the door and opened it for them.

The Major and Jonathon walked inside the gatehouse without delay, and looked around.  There were two main gates, with barely enough space to walk in between them.  The gates were operated by a series of ropes and wooden gears that together made a rather advanced contraption given the medieval structure.  The Major’s gaze panned over the various gears and ropes, trying to pinpoint any weak spots that might bring the gates up at the time of their escape.  Finally, he found one: a small, singular rope that kept the door’s counterweights from falling down and lifting the doors.  If that rope was cut, the counterweights would fall in place and the door would open of its own free will. 

 

The Major led Jonathon outside, and was pleased to see that a few inches of the rope, however small, were visible from the courtyard.  However, there was an unfortunate setback to this idea.

 

“You see that rope up there on the left?” the Major whispered to Jonathon.  “That’s the gate’s only weakness.  If that rope is cut, the contraption will start up and the doors will open, see?”  The boy nodded his understanding, so the Major continued.  “So if we need to get out of here in a hurry, we may need you to sever it from a distance with an arrow.  Think you can do that?”  The boy nodded again, in a way that made it seem like he thought it would be relatively easy.  Unfortunately, there was a setback the Major had yet to tell him.  “However, all of the other ropes next to it will do the opposite, disconnecting the gates from the mechanism and sealing them completely.  You’d have to be sure not to hit any of them, or we’d be trapped.”  He turned to look at Jonathon.  “Okay?”

 

The boy nodded, this time with slightly less confidence, but luckily with enough extra determination to make do without it.

 

“Good,” the Major concluded, “Now let’s keep moving.”
