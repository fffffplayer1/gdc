Chapter 67

 

 

Sparkwing did her job, and carried them all the way to the Mosaic Jesters’ tower in record time, if there were such a record for flying atop a dragon.  They were within breathing distance of the tower by nightfall.  They stopped and hovered in midair about a mile or two from the tower, pondering their next move.

 

“Idagget said we should start at the first three levels,” Praxer muttered, “But he also said they wouldn’t have anything we’d be looking for, and that we should aim for levels eight and nine.”

 

“And those are the lowest military-reserved levels,” Reyna continued for him, “And since you want to find their main server, that’s probably too low.”

 

“Maybe we should try to find a way inside before we think of that,” Axolc said, “It’s rather chilly out here, and I’m sure people praised for being so technologically advanced must have air conditioning.”

 

“Axolc, we need to decide where to enter it first,” Reyna said, rolling her eyes, “We’re figuring out where we want to start.”

 

“Well then, I have an idea,” Axolc piped, “Why don’t we start at the bottom, and enter through the front door?”

 

Praxer and Reyna both stared at him like he was crazy, which wasn’t entirely out of the question.  Eventually Praxer spoke up, “You’re just joking as usual, right?”

 

“Actually no,” Axolc replied, as-a-matter-of-factly.  “We should enter through the main gate.  If they let us through, we’ll be able to investigate the first three levels without having to sneak around so much, and then we’ll have time to formulate our next step.”

“And what if they DON’T let us through?” Praxer asked.

 

“Well then,” Axolc said with a smile, “We can always improvise.”

 

“Alright,” Reyna said, “Supposing we did follow this plan, which I might add is a very stupid plan, under what pretense would we say we were entering the tower?”

 

Axolc’s smile broadened.  “Leave that to me,” he said.

 

Reyna was about to open her mouth again, when someone else made the decision.

 

Axolc held fast as Sparkwing took a dive and landed on the ground.  The dragon grunted something Axolc found hard to make out, but easily understood the gist of.

 

“Sparkwing says she’s too tired to fly any longer.  Though she would love to carry us as far up the tall metal rock as we would like, the three of us together weigh too much, and we’ve already taken quite a lot out of her.”  He stroked the dragon gently.  “She… needs to rest.”

 

“Perfect,” Praxer muttered.  “Now we’ll have to go with that stupid unworkable plan.  Axolc, I hope you know what you’re doing.”

 

“Trust me,” he replied with a smile.

 

“Somehow, that only makes me more worried,” Praxer confessed.

 

***

 

The sun beat down like a wall of pain on the Major and Jonathon as they walked down the rocky canyon road, covering them in an infinite blanket of unbearable heat that made them weary without any effort.  It was past midday, and the Major was liking the environment worse by the moment.

 

Suddenly the canyon walls sloped down and fell away, leaving them open on every side.  The Major looked out at what lay before him, and cursed.

“The desert,” he muttered, looking out at an endless sea of sand.  “This is why no one bothered to pursue us.  They know this is far more dangerous.”  He looked at Jonathon.  “The desert can take more lives than a thousand armies.  It is scorching hot in the day, and freezing cold at night.  Even when you’re prepared for it, the odds of crossing it alive are very slim.  And we aren’t prepared in any sense of the word.”

 

Jonathon nodded grimly.  He looked back at the path behind them, the unsaid question plain.

 

“Even if we could go back, they’ll be waiting for us,” the Major answered, “And besides, we’d be goners before we were halfway there.  We have to press forward, and hope for the best.”

 

Jonathon nodded, and faced the desert once more.  The Major did the same.

 

The desert was massive and stretched on past the horizon in every direction.  There were no trees, no rocks, and nothing but sand.  Lots and lots of sand.  An entire world of nothing but sand.  The sand rose and fell in endless, sloping dunes, like frozen waves on a yellow-white ocean.  In the farthest horizons the color of the sky blurred with the color of the sand and became orange-red, which made it feel like being trapped inside a broiling hot oven that stretched on infinitely. 

 

“…If we just stand here all day, we’ll pass out before we even begin,” the Major eventually said.  Jonathon nodded in reply.

 

The Major felt something touch his hand, and looked down.  Jonathon had grasped it meekly in his own, still staring straight out at the desert.  The Major rotated his hand to hold the boy’s, and held on firmly.  Jonathon looked up, and their eyes met.

 

Then, as if reaching a unanimous decision in the silence, they stepped forward into the barren land of nature’s cruelty.

***

 

Axolc strode up to the gate, with Praxer and Reyna warily following close behind.  People of all sorts were coming and going through the entrance, and completely filled the areas in and around it.  Workers, gardeners, and even a few tourists formed a large crowd in at the gate, and blocked off the view to the inside.

 

“See,” Axolc whispered, “The gate is so busy, no one will notice if we slip through.  The guards aren’t paying attention to anyone outside their reach.”

 

To reinforce this point, Axolc strode into the crowd, and walked right through the gate without anyone noticing.  Reyna and Praxer followed, making sure they walked with purpose as Axolc had.  No one, guard or civilian, tried to stop them or even noticed them in such a diverse crowd.

 

“Not a very competent way of doing their job,” Reyna muttered, “Add to that the sloppy guards we met at the territorial border, and I find it hard to believe these clowns managed to hold out as long as they did.  Weren’t their enemies supposed to be the greatest warriors in history?”

 

“They were,” Praxer said, “But don’t let them hear you saying that.  The Jesters have possession of the Imagination Nexus, and that gives them a major advantage.  With that alone, they could outlast even an infinite siege.”

 

“So the Bat Lords need them to leave this tower in order to win,” Reyna said, “And the Jesters are too cowardly to do so.”

 

“Well, if you think about it…” Axolc said, trying to sound philosophical for a change, “There really is no reason for them not to be cowards, or rather, for them to be brave.  They have everything they need here, and in a way, I think they could have stopped fighting the Bats long ago if they had let go of their hate.”

 

“But they didn’t,” Reyna replied, “And they’re still cowards.”

“Okay guys, enough of that!” Praxer cut the conversation short.  “Pay attention!  We’ve just stepped deep into hostile-controlled territory.  Look around.”

 

Axolc readily complied, but what he saw did not impress him.  At first glance, the level of technology did not far surpass any of the places he had already seen; but on further examination, he realized this was not entirely true.  They were in the center of a bustling city that closely resembled the Bone Village, except for one subtle detail: when Axolc looked towards the horizon, he swore the city was surrounded by a wall.  It was hard to place, because the distance was so great that his eyes, even with the aid of his HUD, could barely detect anything that far away, but when he compared the blurred color of the sky to the color of the wall behind him, he knew he was right, despite how well the air in between him and the other wall disguised this fact.  When he looked up, he realized there was a similar effect far in the air above him, with the exception of what appeared to be artificial lighting.  Though the wall behind him seemed to fade away in the distance to his left and right, he was still close enough to tell that there was a definite inward curve to it, which judging from architectural stereotype was probably a consistent circle that looped all the way around the city. 

 

In summary, with the exception of the gate they had passed through, the city was completely enclosed inside the tower, but the sheer magnitude of the distances was so great that it would be impossible to detect this without major scrutiny, making it easy to forget you weren’t outside.

“Wow,” Axolc half-whispered.  “This is… big.”

 

“We’re on the first level now,” Praxer reaffirmed, “We can only hide in plain sight up to level three.  After that, we’ll have to switch to old fashioned sneaking around the corners, hiding in the shadows, and simply not being seen at all mode of stealth.”

 

“How do you know that?” Reyna inquired.

 

“Idagget told us, remember?”

 

“Oh, right.  Hey, Now that we’re in, why not give him another call?”

 

Praxer shrugged, and they stepped off to the side of the crowd.  They stopped at the mouth of the alley, and leaned back against the walls, casually settling themselves in.  When he was convinced no one was watching, Praxer took out his communicator and switched it on.

 

“BZZRZRG---FINALLY!!!  What happened?!  I’ve been trying to contact you for days!!!”

 

Praxer winced.  “Sorry Idagget,” he said, “We ran into some interference at the border crossing; I think the Jesters must have jammed our signal.  I accidentally left it off after that though, sorry.”

 

“The Jesters couldn’t have blocked our connection!” Idagget protested, “It operates on a wavelength that’s beyond their scientific reach!”

 

“Well, apparently, it isn’t anymore,” Praxer asserted, “Because something blocked our connection, and they were the only ones there…”

 

“Inconceivable!” Idagget stammered, shaking his head from side to side.

“Actually,” Axolc thought for a minute, “Don’t you think if the Jesters were able to block this connection, it would still be blocked for as long as we were in their territories?  And even assuming they aren’t trying to block us here, wouldn’t our connection have to go through the same wall of interference to get to Idagget from here as from the outskirts?”

 

“Hmm… Axolc has a point,” conceded Reyna.  “Since they don’t use this frequency, if they were able to block it, there’s no real reason why they wouldn’t do so here, in the heart of their systems.”

 

“Okay, maybe so…” Praxer admitted.  “But what if they do use this frequency, or better yet, if the Bat Lords did?  Then they’d have a real reason to block it.”

 

Idagget laughed.  “I told you, this technology is beyond their reach.  And the Bat Lords don’t use any form of radio communications whatsoever; their technology is rather medieval.”

 

“Hey,” Reyna whispered, interrupting the conversation, “We have a watcher.”
