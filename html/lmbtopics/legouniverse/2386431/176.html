~~Emergency transmission to Vanda Darkflame, suddenly and inexplicably cut off short

 

 

Chapter 72

 

The Major woke up to a whirlwind of sand in his eyes.  He blinked groggily, wondering how he had managed to sleep through it, until he realized he was already half-buried in sand. 

 

The adrenal panic kicking him awake, the Major frantically tried to stand up, but was buffeted left and right by the sandstorm that had risen during the night.  Where was Jonathon…?

 

Oh no.

 

“JONATHON?!!?!!” he cried out, but he heard no reply.  He couldn’t even hear the sound of his own screams; they were all drowned out in the hurricane of sand.

 

Desperately, the Major started panning the ground with his hands, searching desperately for any sign of the boy.  The whishing sands stung his eyes and face, even through his helmet.  “JONATHON!!!!!!” he cried out again, unheard by even himself in that roaring din.  He got up and attempted to run off in search of the boy, but the most he could do was slowly trudge through the sand and wind, relentlessly buffeted and tossed about. 

 

He couldn’t see him.  He couldn’t see ANYTHING.  He held up his own hand in front of his face, and he couldn’t even see that.  “JONATHON!!!!!!  No-no-no-no-no-no-no…”

‎02-13-2014 12:57 PM

The Major’s panicked muttering led him into a trance, until the winds of the sandstorm which had kept him wandering aimlessly finally decided to stop toying with him, and pushed back at him with redoubled force.  As they further reduced the meager fruit of his efforts to less than a crawl, the fatigue finally caught up with him and dragged him to the ground.  He didn’t know how long he lay there, face-down in the sand, weakly muttering, unable to move, as a few lone tears slowly rolled down his sand-stung eyes, before someone grabbed him by the scruff of his neck and hauled him to his feet.

 

The figure led him a little ways in one direction before he stopped and bent down and searched the sand for something with his hands.  Suddenly the figure pushed down, and the sand in front of them fell away into a pitch-black hole, the edges of which were unnaturally straight.  The figure led the Major down a series of steps into the black hole before them.

 

As they reached the bottom of the stairs, the Major heard the iron door close behind them, and a series of bright overhead lights came on.  Now that the Major could see his rescuer, he smiled.

 

“Jonathon, you never cease to amaze me,” he said proudly.  “How did you know this was here?”

 

The boy shrugged, as if to say it didn’t matter and there were other questions that did.  The Major nodded in reply.

 

It was at that moment that the Major realized, even though the boy still didn’t talk directly, the Major was able to read his expressions and emotions, so they were able to converse anyways, and had been doing so for quite some time.  This thought nearly brought tears to the Major’s eyes.  The boy was looking more hopeful every day.

But he had dwelt on this enough.  The Major turned away and scanned the room they were in.  By luck or fate, they had stumbled upon an underground bunker in the desert, hidden underneath the sand like a needle in a haystack.  The walls, floor, and ceiling were made of steel, and multiple panels on the ceiling lit the room with an eerie light.  They appeared to be in the main room, which was comfortably large, though its only furnishing was a messy workbench in the opposite-right corner.  There were two doors: one on the left wall, and one on the wall across from them, next to the workbench.  The Major went to inspect the other rooms.

 

The door on the left led to a smaller room that appeared to be lodgings, if the four bunks lining the walls were any indication.  Apart from that, the room was vacant, so the Major let and went to inspect the other room.

 

When he saw what was inside, his heart skipped a beat. 

 

An entire store of food and water- enough to last over a year- was inside.

Reyna had almost finished sawing her way through the first bar when she realized her mistake.  Though he may not have noticed the quiet back-and-forth scraping sound she had made getting this far, the guard would certainly notice the loud snap that would occur if she finished it.  Plus, the bars were spaced out closely enough that she would have to saw through at least three before she could squeeze through.  Hmm…

 

She looked at the guard again.  He was definitely smarter than the last guard, but how much smarter exactly…?  Now might be a good time to find out.

“Excuse me, guard?!” she called out.  “This cell is flawed!”

 

“Shut up!” the guard replied, still absorbed in his game.  “Our cells are flawless by design; they’re unbreakable!”

 

“Oh yeah?  Then explain this:” Reyna pointed down at her handiwork.

 

The guard raised his eyes a notch with a disinterested stare, but when he saw the place with only a small fraction of the original bar remaining, his eyes grew wide, and he dashed over to investigate.  When he investigated the bar up close and saw that his eyes had not deceived him, he panicked.

 

“WHA-what do I do?!” he shouted in fear.

 

“Well…” Reyna replied, tilting her head back as if thinking.  “You could… move me one cell to the right…?”

 

Though panicked, this guard was not quite so easily fooled.  “I’m… not sure I have the authority to do that…” he protested doubtfully.

 

“Well then, I’m glad you’ll be the one they’ll blame when your superior comes down here and sees his most dangerous prisoner in a defective cell, when there were six more in perfect condition right next to it!”

 

Now the guard’s expression went dark.  “I think you’re trying to manipulate-”

 

Instead of letting him finish his sentence, Reyna gave the bar that final kick it needed to spring loose, and it snapped outward in half like a broken guitar string.  All the while she kept her gaze focused on the guard, and she was glad she did, because the terrified expression on his face after she illustrated her point was priceless.

 

He ran back to the other side of the room to retrieve the keys.  “I’ll just… move you one cell to your left,” he said, as if changing the direction somehow gave him the last word.  He came back with the keys, and deftly unlocked her cell door.

The moment he swung open the door, Reyna socked him in the face. 

 

“FINALLY!!!” she huffed.  She had spent way too long in that cell for her comfort.  Sure, it had been fun messing with the guards, but she had things to do, places to go, people to meet, and she also wanted to give Droavk a good beat-down.  She doubted it would affect the timestream so long as she didn’t smash him…. Besides, she doubted he even had any future descendents that she had to worry about; what kind of girl would be crazy enough to marry that monster?!

 

After feeling the sweet relief of donning her armor again, she stashed the prone guard in her cell and locked the door.  She then put the keys in her pocket and finally left that dreadful room.

 

***

 

Supreme Lord Alkara walked into his office and sat down at his desk.  Shortly afterwards, Zanjor entered- without knocking, because that would be too prudent and polite than he was capable of being- and sat down in front of him. 

 

“So, Zanjor,” Alkara began, “It has been five days since you tried to usurp me, and drove away a potential ally in the process.  You have been avoiding me ever since, and I have let you do so, not out of mercy, but out of disgust for the sight of your face.  Now you come to me unannounced, yet on peaceful terms.  Something must be troubling you.”

 

Zanjor looked confused.  “Unannounced?  I gave you three hours’ notice I was coming.”

 

“I mean you didn’t knock before you entered,” Alkara explained.  “Now what’s on your mind?”

‎02-13-2014 01:00 PM

Zanjor looked down, and it was then that Alkara noticed he was holding something in his hand: a scroll.  How curious…

 

“My lord… you and I have never been on equal terms, but… something has come up that I believe takes precedence over our own quarrels.”

 

Alkara raised an eyebrow, and held out his hand.  Zanjor handed him the scroll, and Alkara unfurled it and read its contents.  His face grew dark.

 

“Rally the troops,” he ordered.  “This must be met with force.” 

 

Zanjor nodded with a bow, and left to go raise the army.  Alkara stayed in his office, holding the curled parchment with a tight grip, frowning in thought, causing creases to form on his forehead. 

 

There was trouble on the outskirts.  An army of Mosaic Jesters had set foot in the neutral zone, led by one of their highest generals.  This was not someone who would act without orders, which meant that, at last, they had stopped cowering in fear in their little tower, and had come out ready for a fair fight.

 

Normally, that would be a good thing, but still it troubled him.  This was not the Mosaic Jester way.  They tended to avoid pitched battles, using hit and run tactics, often targeting supply routes or resource-oriented targets rather than risk storming an actual military base.  And they never, ever violated the treaty without some sort of excuse already planned out.

 

Alkara realized he was daydreaming, and shook himself awake.  Fine.  If General Droavk was going to openly and directly violate the treaty, then Alkara would repay in kind.  The time for politics had passed.  This meant war.
