Chapter 6: Truths Discovered: Part 4

>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>

      “We were all at our village.  A group of rebels had decided to stay there for the night, hiding in houses and barns.  We all knew that harboring rebels for a night was dangerous for the village, but we figured that it would be alright for one night.  They would leave soon in the morning.  But then, that morning, a group of Falsohk that had been following the rebels attacked the village.  A look-out spotted them minutes before they were upon us, and all of the able-bodied men and women, and some who weren't, rushed to the rebel camp where they were handing out guns and other weapons.  We all took up stations in our houses, on the roofs and in the fields, wherever there was a bit of cover.  We had heard what had happened to other villages that had been stormed, how once they had left there was nothing but ashes and smashed bodies.  We weren't going to let that happen to us." Cont.

A group rode to the centre of the village and began to direct the others, pointing at different houses and shouting orders. We were waiting for the signal to begin the ambush, and as soon as we heard the whistle, we began to fire upon them. I was in the front room of our house, firing through the window, with my wife beside me. Our son was still at the village school, and we hoped that he would stay there. As I already said before, my daughter was away. As soon as the Falsohk reallized that we were still in the village, they started to fight back. The fight was hard. We had no place to send our wounded men, and they were falling fast. I saw my neighbour fall from my roof into the dust, shot down, and cries and shouts filled the air. The Falsohk began burning the houses, trying smoke everyone out, beggining at the end of our row and slowly making their way closer. Another group began to make its way the in the other direction, setting the houses on fire, and I began to fear for the children in the school, for they were growing closer to it. Shelters started to collapse as the fire consumed them, and some of the villagers had no choice but to run out into the street." The sentry looked back into the flames, as if he could see the scene echoing over again in the flames.

I've really, really been neglecting this story, but I got past my writers block, so here's a new part, and I'll try to be a lot faster. . .

 

Chapter 6: Truths Discovered: Part 5
>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>
     I waited for the man to continue, sitting as still as I could.  I looked over at his face, the orange light from the fire flickering over his scruffy, lightly-wrinkled face.  The moon, slowly passing over us, glimmered down through the darkness, its light lost in the fog surrounding us.  The man continued, looking back over at me.
     "There was dust everywhere, thick dust, mixed with smoke, rising in huge clouds.  You couldn't see ten feet.  Just see white lasers, piercing the dust, and flames, rising high in the sky.  Then I saw a figure, running fast, coughing, stumbling.  It was my son.  He ran in the door and dropped to the ground.
    " 'What are you doing here?' I said, rushing over to him.  His face was covered in dust.  'Are you alright,'  I asked.  He nodded.
     'They got the school,' he said, tears streaming down his face.  'Talao didn't make it,' he said.  Talao was one of his friends, a girl."
     I knew I had heard the name Talao before, in my brain the name registered, but it wouldn't, it couldn't bring up a face.  It was as if my brain wouldn't let me remember this person.  This had happened before, though, so I just ignored it.
     "What happened after that?" I asked the sentry.
     "I told him to go down to the cellar, where he would be safe, but he didn't go.  He said, 'I have to go back to the school, the teacher needs me to help him get the rest of the kids out of the village to the cave shelter.'  He hugged me quickly, hugged his mother, said a quick, 'Take care of yourselves,' and then rushed out the door before I could stop him, grabbing up my neighbor's gun that was still lying in our doorway.  I rushed to the doorway, trying to stop him."   Cont.

     Cont . . . "I shouted after him as I watched him disappear through the dust heading back the way he had come.  He quickly ducked into the doorway of the first house he came to, and fired a few shots toward two or three soldiers coming down the street."
     The man looked up at me, the great sorrow in his eyes extremely apparent.  I could feel his pain through his eyes, and my heart ached.
     "I should have stopped him," the man said, turning back to the fire, "But I knew I couldn't.  He had it set in his heart to go save those kids, and nothing could have stopped him.  I saw him disappear in the dust and smoke, and that was the last time I saw him."
     I looked into the flames myself, and I could see a younger version of this man, standing in a doorway, staring through clouds of smoke, trying to catch a last glimpse of his son as he disappeared into the chaos of a war.  Suddenly, something in my mind tries to register, and I see before my eyes; a blurry glimpse of dust and smoke, a small flash so fast I could barely make it out.  It happens again, repeating again and again, getting clearer and clearer.  'What is happening to me,' I ask myself, and then more appears, a flash of a man's face,  the glint of a visor, a building in front of me.  My eyes widen as I slowly realize what is happening, what this means, what I am seeing, and I look up at the man in front of me.  A name, shouted, keeps echoing in my ears, over and over, 'Miltiades, Miltiades, Miltiades.'
     "Miltiades," I whispered.  The sentry looked up, his eyes wide.
     "What did you say?" he asked.
     "Miltiades."  I said it louder this time.
     "That was my son’s name," the man said, looking at me, a sudden realization apparent in his eyes.  "How could you know?"  I just looked at him, not seeing the movement, not hearing the sound until it was too late.
>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>
