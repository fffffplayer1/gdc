  . . .After anything was finished, it was supposed to be taken to the testing bay to make sure everything was working properly.  Testing was another long task, and he wasn't even sure how to test an escape capsule.  He looked over at the test bay and winced, knowing that he would miss his lunch if he had to test the capsule.  “It's not like it's that important,” he told himself, “I know I got all the wiring hooked up correctly.”  Even so, he knew it would be wrong and that he would be severely punished if his error was ever found out, so he carefully drove the trolley over to the test bay.  "I'll do a electrical test to make sure that it turns on, and then I'll go and have my lunch,” he said as he quickly hooked it up and turned the generator on.  The capsule began to hum and lights began to flash.  “Good, it works,” the technician said, relieved that he wouldn’t have to open it back up and do more work.  He quickly unplugged it and wheeled it over to be put back into the ship.  He didn't know that just because something turns on doesn't mean that it's okay.  Because it wasn't okay. It was seriously flawed.  But that doesn't matter, does it?

Chapter 7: Beyond Our Bars: Part 1
>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>
     Pain.  I felt pain again.  It reminded me of that day, years ago, when I joined the Falsohk.  When I woke up in that room.  I slowly opened my eyes.  I could feel movement.  A dim, blue light was shining down on me as I looked around.  Four walls, no windows, a small door.  The sound of wind rushing past.  The air felt hot and thick.  I try to get up, to go over to the door, but I can't.  I look down at myself and find that I am strapped to the gurney that I am lying on.  Thick metal bands criss-cross my arms and legs, holding me down.  'What happened?'  I ask myself.  Then I remember.  'My dad.  I found my dad.  After all this time, I thought he was dead, that I was the only one left.  And now he's gone.'
     "Fenloe," I seethed.  "He told me I was the only one left.  He told me that the rebels had defeated my father.  It was him all along, lying to me."  I pulled at my bands, trying to break free, but they wouldn't budge.  I lay back and sighed.  'How long was I going to be here?'  I thought.  'Where was here?'  I looked around again.  I was obviously traveling, as indicated by the wind rushing past the walls, making a dull roar.  Mostly likely I was aboard a airship traveling back to either the Romulus or the Remus, inside one of the compartments near the engine.  That was why it was so hot.  I heard someone walk past the door.  There must be a hallway or something there, I thought.  I wondered how long it had been since I had seen Fenloe defeat my father.  For all I knew it could have been years ago.  I heard more steps outside my door; they stopped and I heard the small sharp beeps of a keypad.  I looked up at the ceiling as the doors slid open and and two footsteps echoed in my small compartment. . . Cont.

       "Troy," a man said.  It was Fenloe.  I didn't respond; just stared upwards.  "As you can see, something is wrong with him," I heard Fenloe whisper to another man.  I turned my head at this to see who was there.  To my surprise it was Captain Boleman.
     "Troy," the Captain said, considerably louder.  I still didn't respond, after all, my name wasn't Troy.  I was Miltiades.
     "Where did you say you found him?" asked the Captain.
     "One of my men found him out in the desert; lying in the shade of a rock near the enemy's camp.  He knew who it was and reported it back to me, and I personally took a few men out to get him.  He acted violent upon our approach though; and I could tell there was something deeply wrong with him.  Once we got back to our camp with him, our company doctor took a look at him and pronounced him 'Mentally Ill'.  I brought him back here as soon as I could, but it seems there is nothing that can be done."  Cont.

     "Do you have a written paper from the doctor who pronounced him mentally ill?"
     "Yes, right here," Fenloe said, handing him a paper containing the doctor's seal.  Captain Boleman scanned it carefully, then turned his attention back to me.
     "Very well, you have my permission to do away with him, as long as you let him get his affairs in order first," the Captain said, turning from the room and walking out the door.
     "What?"  I said, swiftly, whirling my head to see the Captain.  Unfortunately, no sound came from my mouth, the nerve suppressor wouldn't allow my vocal cords to vibrate.  Fenloe smiled at me, a cruel, heartless smile, as two men in white jumpsuits came in and took hold of the gurney I was strapped to, one on each side.  Fenloe turned and walked forward down the hall, and my gurney floated after him on a path to destruction.  I looked at the men on either side of me, at their faceless helmets, each step bringing me closer, closer to the brightly lit corridor composed of twelve airtight gas chambers, six on each side.

Chapter 7: Beyond Our Bars: Part 2

>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>
       Green lights glittered along the corridor in strips along the floor; I could see them growing longer behind me in each step.  I pulled at the straps holding down my arms but it seemed as if they only grew tighter.  The heavy sound of Fenloe's boots above my head echoed in my ears, pounding in my head, a steady tramp towards what seemed would be the end of the road.  We turned a corner and I knew that we had arrived.  Fenloe walked around to my side and sneered down at me. He pushed a button on my chest, just as he had done many years ago,  and though I knew that it was artificial, that I was about to die no matter how I felt, I felt a calm absorb me, a peace that took me over.
     "You can't do this, you can't just kill people like this," I breathed, feeling my eyes starting to close. Cont.

"I smash people all the time on the battlefield, it would be impossible to count the bodies I have smashed.   We and everyone that has ever lived are all just robots anyway, it does not matter what we do, we're all going to end up at the same place anyway.  Some people just get to the end quicker than others.  We are basically just pieces of organic matter blundering around in a cage called Life, and no one knows what lies beyond the bars.  Soon you will know, though, you will know what no man knows. . ."  I felt as if I were falling now, the words echoing in my head, falling as bright lights danced around me like fireflies on a summer night on Tanis, blues, yellows, reds, swirling around, slowly mixing together into a bright white glow that surrounded me.  I faintly felt my arms being undone from the straps, now my legs as well.  I heard the echoey swish of a door as it slid open and I was picked up from the gurney and dropped to the floor inside the chamber.
     "Farewell, Troy, as you begin your journey," I heard Fenloe call to me as the doors slid close once again and the gas began to seep in slowly, a green smog flowing through the air.  "You will know what no man knows or will ever know. . ."

Chapter 7: Beyond Our Bars: Part 3
>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>
     As soon as the door was closed, I opened my eyes and looked around me. I was lying on the hard metal floor, the walls were made of plates of metal as well, airtight.  A green smog was coming out of multiple holes in the walls near the floor, and it flowed toward me, rolling and hissing like a blanket of cobras.  I knew it wouldn't be too long until the whole chamber was full of the suffocating fog; I would be killed within 10 minutes if I didn't act fast.  I didn't know much about the different gases and things used in chambers like these; on the battlefields and in training I hadn't been exposed to things like this.  I could see that the fog seemed to stay on the floor, it was heavier than whatever else was filling the room.  Maybe If I got high enough, I would be able to get enough air to outlast the fog.  The ceiling was pretty high, about ten feet, that could be high enough.  How could I get up there?  I could see by the nail scratches and dents on the walls that many unfortunate had tried to climb the walls before, the walls were obviously too smooth.  Even if I could get up there, who knew how long it would take before the fog dissipated.  The only way to avoid would be to escape. As I looked carefully around, I suddenly knew what to do.
>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>
