Starless Night

Introduction

‘Life’

The beginning.

This is, of course, a subject of much controversy. For life started once, at a time so long ago that history hadn’t been written. A time before voices of different tones, words of different lengths.

Some say that it all began with a single, omnipotent and all-powerful being. A deity- or several- with such power that he (or they) created the entire universe. This is, of course, flawed, for what was there to make these great beings in the first place?

Others say that time molded things into how they are now. Billions and trillions of years, atom after atom, molecule after molecule, living creature after living creature.

But still, the beginning isn’t known. By rights, there shouldn’t have been a very beginning, because something must have begun before it to spark it. We will probably never learn the truth of how it all started- how life gave birth to the first.

So I will start many years later- millions, in fact, after evolution or the craftsmanship of gods. For our story, well, it doesn’t necessarily begin and the very beginning.

War. If there was a start to all things, it was there from that point. Bickering, greed, corruption, anger, sorrow, power. But there were also the other things, good things. Love. Compassion. Sacrifice.

They, no matter how it was all made, were there. And they always conflicted, yet they couldn’t live without each other.

Our story starts with war. The war that, in a way, sparked the beginning.

The Nation, embodiment of all things good, was failing. The grand and ancient civilization of the Seekers failing as its people twisted agonizingly across the Homeworld, being slaughtered by the thousands.

The Shaa’Dao had come.

A demonic army from the Nightmare Lands, bred from the fears and sorrows of all that lived, molded by the darkness that was power, hate, tragedy.

The Nation didn’t stand a chance. All it stood for, love and compassion, was quickly being drowned out by the screams of the dying, by the tears of the anguished, by the corrupted mastery of their once kind rulers.

It had started as a search for power, an expedition to find the source of all things good, the Nriisha Lumnii. With four great maps set down by their ancestors, the explorers set out to find the Lumnii.

Sadly, they succeeded. The entire Nation rushed to the ancient, cubic world of Anaura, often called ‘The Heart’, and fought over the marvelous and awesome power granted by the Nriisha Lumnii. And where there is fighting, where there is killing, where there is power… there is always the Shaa’Dao, the eternal evil.

The Nation, sparked by their internal squabbling for higher ranking, brought the Starless Night, the nightmare, the forever sleep, upon themselves. Their greed was their downfall.

And that is where our story… begins.

~A Very Long Time Later (Numbers unknown)- 28 years BSN (Before Second Night)

~Planet S’haitn, Outer Edge

 

It was raining. Sheets of water bulleted to the ground, droplets pattering off roofs and gathering into bulging tears on the edge of leaves, dripping down to the terra-firma beneath. Little frozen spheres of hail came hurtling down from the heavens to shatter upon the cobbles or gather in the bleak patches of over-watered grass. Puddles splattered, spraying the little wet particles across the churning street.

A boot- thick, weatherworn, black as pitch and reaching a quarter way up his leg- hit one such puddle with a splash, and as it pulled itself up to continue walking, it left with a squelching sound, trailing sticky mug behind it.

The man to whom this boot belonged was cloaked, his billowing cape- just as black as his liquid-proofed shoes- whipped about his slender form, rippling in the air as the blistering hail and icy struck it. His hood was pulled low over his head, obscuring his features in a shadow that was further blurred by the curtain of rain.

But if someone looked at him more closely, one would notice a long, obsidian-colored staff tucked beneath his right armpit, his waving cloak occasionally wrapping around its dark, wooden body. Just a plain stick, with no ornaments to be seen.

The man reached out with a gloved hand, a finger pressing lightly in the doorbell. A sound much like a gong clanging down a mountainside echoed through the house he was standing before, causing him to wince slightly beneath his hood. He took a step back, his eyes trailing up and down the building, considering it keenly, although his head seemed not to move a centimeter.

It was rather tall and looked quite ragged- almost like a cardboard box, in fact, without any notable details. It had no windows, and, in the bleak weather, its long shadow, somewhat distorted by the extensive, recently made ponds, stretched over the waiting minifigure ominously.

The door swung open, the groping wind and rain gushing into the newfound hole with such vigor that the man outside actually slid forward a few inches.

“Who is it!?” Came a voice from within the building, hardly reaching a high enough volume to penetrate the screaming gales.

“An old friend!” The cloaked minifigure replied, giving a slight bow that caused his cape to whirl over his head impressively.

He then walked in, and the other person quickly shut the door, sliding a bolt into place and firmly locking it.

The minifigure in the hood, who was quite drenched, took a great big shake, wetting the floor and forming yet another miniscule pool beneath his feet.

“Extinguish the hearth fire, why don’t you?” The man who had let him in spat derisively, taking a quick, almost rodent-like step back from the flying drops.

Since the room with bathed with so much bright light, it took a while for the hooded man to reorient himself. When he did, he turned to look at the other minifigure.

He wore a lab coat which, one, might have been the proud bearer of the color ‘white’, but, over time and many stains, had become more of the yellowish green that comes from a sick cat’s gullet. He had plastic doctor’s gloves (Which were blank in color), and had long, salt-and-pepper hair which stuck straight up, as if he had encountered a bit too much electricity. But the strangest thing on him would have been the goggles- magnificently made ones that glowed a bright, light blue where his eyes should have been visible.

“Now.”, the scientist- for of course, that’s what he was- mumbled, peering disdainfully at his floor, which was glimmering with water. “I was you again, who are you?”

“And I tell you again.” The hooded man smiled, “An old friend.”

“I am not up for a riddle game right now. I’m busy, now, please go away. I have got no rooms for visitors. I can give you an umbrella if you like.”

The minifigure in the black cloak, which wasn’t billowing now that there was no wind, laughed. “Not up for a riddle game? Really? That’s new- and since when did you not have any rooms! My friend, I think I should know you well enough to remember that you are the most excellent host.”

The scientist seemed taken aback, his fair face- which seemed made for laughing- suddenly curling up into a slight frown, his goggles eyes shining weirdly. Then he noticed the plain black staff under the stranger’s arm.

He gasped, his face quickly morphing into a beaming grin. “Bally! How do you do, my utterly distracting cohort?”

‘Bally’ flung off his hood, spraying even more muddy water to the ground, and even peppering it with a few hail stones. His face as pale and long, and he sported a dashing, curled mustache, shoulder length, oily black hair, and a dark goatee on his pointed chin. “How many times do I have to tell you not to call me Bally, Zyzzyva?”

Zyzzyva snorted. “Once more, Balthazar, once more.”                                        

Balthazar smiled a warm smile, his lips almost taking his ears. “How have you been doing, Zyz? How’s the Industries been fairing you?”

Zyzzyva’s face darkened. “The parochial ascendancy shut me down.” He muttered grimly, the long and complicated words that were his trademark pouring from his mouths as simply as one would spit out foul-tasting water.

Balthazar gasped. “Those authorities! If I could give them a piece of my mind-”

“You’d be dead and they’d be disgusted, holding a fragment of your brain.” Zyzzyva interjected.

Balthazar blinked, then barked out a laugh. “When you put it that way, I guess you’re right.”

When there was no reply, he continued. “Anyways, you must be wondering why I’ve come-”

“I’m always wondering, in case you’ve noticed.”

“Alright then. But I went travelling, and I’ve found-”

“Shht!” Zyzzyva hushed harshly, “Talk later! You must be famished, hankering, covetous, craving! Simply avid- esurient, I say! Come come, you must gormandize! I will get the stove ready.”

And with that, Balthazar was shoved unceremoniously into a tall and intricately designed chair before a small, round table, his staff in a large can along with a few umbrellas and his dripping cloak hanging on one of the many hooks.

From the outside, the house looked like something that had dropped from the rump of a constipated cow. But the inside was another matter completely.

It wasn’t lavish, no no. Not at all. It was… wisecracking. Yes, there were plenty of fine carpets and flowers and silken curtains, but it was also covered in hundreds of wacky inventions. Several robotic birds whizzed about his head, chirping metallic chirps, and a self-powered train buzzed about on its rail. At one point, a hatch opened in its side and sprouted several legs, and it hoisted itself, leaving the track to climb up one of the table’s legs like a caterpillar. In moments, it was on Balthazar’s lap, curled up and ‘purring’.

There were clocks all along the walls, ticking, tocking and ‘cookooing’. Several candles hovered about on little, floating contraptions, and books and blueprints were cluttered all over the floor, some dangerously close to the black-stone hearth and its warm, crackling fire.

Balthazar was so absorbed that he didn’t notice someone had crept up behind him before it spoke, startling him from his reverie.

“Uncle Typhonus!”

She was quite small, wearing a black pashmina turtle-neck sweater to keep out the cool of the stormy night. Along with that, she wore an equally dark skirt- and not a tutu, mind you. It wasn’t billowing at all- it was quite flat actually, and went all the way down to her bare feet, which were hidden beneath it.

Balthazar bent down to face his niece, grinning warmly. “How are you, my little shadow-stalker?”

Yes, he said shadow-stalker. Vanda was an intelligent girl, and, when she wished, she could become almost invisible (Not literally, of course), able to sneak up on a blood hound. So no ‘pumpkin-muffin’ for her, no, absolutely not.

“Absolutely fine, if I may say so myself.” She giggled, hugging him tightly around the shoulders. “But I have also been rather lonely. You know, father’s always working. Ever since he lost his job, he has been putting all his free time into some project which, for some reason, he won’t tell me anything about. I find it quite…” She frowned, scrunching up her nose to find the right word. “Vexatious.” She finished. Then her eyes gave a little, wicked twinkle, and she nudged her uncle playfully. “You wouldn’t know anything about it, would you?”

Balthazar’s grin widened. “Not at all, my dear.”

Vanda gave a very unladylike snort. “Fine, keep your dirty little secrets. I don’t care what nasty things you do with black cats and cauldrons, plagued frogs and the tails of rats.”

Balthazar grabbed her in his strong hands and hoisted her up onto his shoulder. “And don’t forget magic brooms!”

Both of them spun around in circles, laughing until Zyzzyva returned, smiling at the scene and holding a tray full of succulent-smelling meals- along with several new stains on his lab coat.

Typhonus sat Vanda down on one of the high chairs (Without armrests- she simply hated them) before putting himself on the one he had been placed in before. Zyzzyva placed the platters on the table’s circular surface, wondrous, mouth-watering smells wafting into their flaring nasal passages.

And, as you can imagine, they tucked it.

For starters, the trio each had a half of an avocado (Well, Vanda had two. After all, she was beginning to grow!), each missing the massive pit that made its home in the fruit’s greenish flesh. In the hole where the seeds once were, there was a spectacular sauce of lime and honey, and, using teaspoons, they soon scooped out all the sweet-and-sour drenched meat.

Next, Zyzzyva served a marvelous onion soup, that had apparently been cooking for twenty four hours to get the perfect caramelization- it had started as half a kilo of red onions for each person (Yes, that much), and, after simmering for a day, had ended as a water-thin black soup, topped with croutons fried in butter and then rained with grated parmesan.

The soup completely cleared out the sinuses, and all of them felt completely awake (Although their stomachs couldn’t quite handle another bite.

Balthazar Typhonus wiped his mouth with a napkin. “That was incredible.” He muttered, a contented smile stamped across his angular features.

Zyzzyva grinned, slightly tipping his bowl to onto side and scooping up what little was left of the soup, little grated fragments of cheese floating around in it.

Suddenly, there came a sudden gust of wind, blowing several hail stones into a window, making an agitating ‘clank, clank, clank!’. Yes, I said windows. That was another amazing invention on Zyzzyva Darkflame’s part- the window’s couldn’t be seen from the outside, but were clear as daylight from the building’s innards.

“Ghastly weather, isn’t it?” Balthazar murmured under his breath, nodding emphatically to the clinking panes of glass.

Zyzzyva laughed. “Remember, there is no such thing was bad weather.” He grinned wickedly, setting his bowl down, “Only bad clothing.”

All three of them burst out hollering until they spluttered and tears welled up in their reddening eyes.

After all that was done, Balthazar put on his best business-like expression, tapping the top of his un-ornamented scepter on the tabletop and giving a rough cough. “Now, about why I came…”

“Yes, yes!” Vanda clapped her hands together, “Tell us a story! Or whatever you’re going to show- or tell, for that matter!”

Balthazar looked at Vanda sadly, shaking his head. “I’m sorry, Vanda. But…” He looked to his cousin for help.

Zyzzyva nodded. “Vanda, if you please, go to your room.”

“What? But…”

Vanda sighed, shrugged, and got off her chair, leaving her bowl, plate, and cutlery, and left through a side door.

The moment she was done, Zyzzyva burst. “You’ve found it, then?”

Balthazar smiled subtly, his moustache twitching a little with the movement. He got up from his chair, walked to the hook where his black cloak hung, and produced what seemed to be a dimly glowing, elongated, light blue crystal.

Zyzzyva mumbled something incomprehensible to himself, then gasped, pulling his chair forward and patting the table with his hands eagerly.

Balthazar placed the crystal on the table, and all the light in that area fluttered off, the crystal slowly growing brighter as they did so. Zyzzyva whispered some more, the words becoming louder and louder until Typhonus noticed he wasn’t really saying anything, just making sounds.

He knew why.

You see, Zyzzyva wore those goggles for a reason- he was blind. Not blind as a bat, for bats can see quite well. No, he was completely and utterly blind.

Those goggles, however, powered by an extremely rare power source known as ‘Imagination’, allowed him to see the world in a different light. Every time any sound, no matter how minute, was made, the sonar and echolocation devices in the glasses would allow him to ‘see’, and not just hear.

And now he saw this crystal, something that he and his cousin, Balthazar, had been searching for all their lives.

“Where did you find it?” He whispered, his voice strained.

“A treasure hoard in my travels. Apparently, it had been found by a notorious space pirate- I only found what his ship was called: 'The Trailblazer'. But whenever I ask someone about the pirate's name, they get all scared and tell me that it’s bad luck to utter it, and some taboo will bring that buccaneer of a demon after you. Poppycock, if I may say so myself.”

“Hael.” Zyzzyva said.

And what was in the space inside the hoop? Nothing to be seen by a quick glance, but, with a closer inspection, one would notice that the air there seemed to be thicker, almost tangible.
