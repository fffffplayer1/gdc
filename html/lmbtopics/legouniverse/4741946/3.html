~BSN 12

~Planet S’haitn- Outer Edge

 

Zyzzyva Darkflame’s funeral had taken place exactly seventeen years before. The house had been left as a wreckage- it wasn’t cleared away or renovated.

A man stood before it, just as he had once every year since the accident. His cloak, black as pitch, glistened dimly in the light of the setting sun as the droplets of a slight drizzle pattered off of its fluttering surface. He wore a top hat- just as dark in color, with a musty grey ribbon tied around the base.

And in his hand was a tall staff- also obsidian in color. It was smooth, perfectly crafted. And on its top was set a slightly elongated crystal that glowed blue. A memory of that terrible, stormy night seventeen years earlier.

As far as Baron Balthazar Typhonus was concerned, the entire incident was his fault. He had found and brought the ancient artifact to his life-long friend, and it was him who had insisted in the Search for all these years, even though Zyzzyva had given up hope long before. It had started as a sudden strange fancy. A game played by two young children.

But ideas are like the seeds of an oak. They take hold, grow taller and taller, their roots groping deeper into the ground, and they stay there for what seems like forever. Even axes glance off their life-toughened, petrified bark.

And now Zyzzyva was dead. Balthazar wished that it had been him. Him who had stood in front of that infernal machine when the crystal was placed into it. At least then Vanda would have grown up with a proper father.

Vanda. Balthazar remembered her now- a young, happy, bubbly little girl with intelligence far beyond her age. She had always been smiling, that childish, innocent glint crackling in her eyes like a warm hearth. Back when she referred to him as ‘Uncle’.

Baron Typhonus had lost a friend that day. Vanda Darkflame had lost so much more than that. Suffice to say that now, she pinned him down in the darkest corners of the title, ‘Your grace’.

Balthazar had taken the young, anguished ten year old girl in after Zyzzyva’s untimely end. She had been so young. She had felt the impact of it far more than he had. That loving hearth in her eyes had been replaced by wildfire. The kind that goes on and on, swallowing things up and not going out until there isn’t any more kindling. Her childhood had been destroyed.

Typhonus had become a Baron shortly after the incident, helping control the universe within the Ruling Council of the galactic empire known as the Ascension, serving as the leader of its Secret Intelligence, known as the ‘Eye of the Storm’.

Three years later, war broke out. The Ascension was split into two parts: The Iron Law and the Insinuation. Balthazar was a Baron in the Iron Law, a group that was supposedly trying to restore order to the universe, while the Insinuation terrorized hundreds of star systems through assassinations and bombings.

And through it all, he had raised Vanda Darkflame. The jolly girl who had been struck by the hammer of life, and had been forced to create an impenetrable outer shell to protect others from the horrors inside.

Baron Balthazar Typhonus sighed, a tear trailing down his cheek and mixing inkishly with the polluted drizzle. He absently twisted his mustache, then lifted his hat from his shoulder-length, oily hair and bowed shortly to the ashen ruins, before turning on his heel and strutting away glumly.

Vanda never came with him on his annual trip to S’haitn. She never wanted to see the wreckage again.

~Three Days Later, Musket Base, Orbit of Éndiûre, the Hollow Straits

 

The guard chief slumped slowly to the ground, the fall muffled by the strong arms of his attacker.

She stepped over his lifeless body lightly, making not even a ripple of the faintest sound, and slipped the dagger into its sheathe with an equal quiet before making her way forward, step by soundless step.

The metallic footsteps were getting nearer- ‘clink-clink-clink’.

“Attention all! Nothing is wrong, no, absolutely not. Just a little malfunction in the 57th Chute. The ship’s guards- err, engineers will be taking care of it shortly. –bzzzt- So no, the Iron Law isn’t –bzzzt- attacking. Just carry on –bzzt- business, this stuff happens all the time. Thank you for -bzzzzssshhhh-”

The intercom gave a buzz, fizzing out with the sound of a radio leaving its range, before stopping the message with a crack!

Clink-clink-clink!

They were just around the bend now. She pressed herself against the smooth, grey wall, her tight white outfit and the pitch black visor that covered her face hardly camouflaging her position. She fingered the little, portable and hand-held console lightly, her thumb tapping the red button on top with excruciating care.

“He $h0u|d be ju$t @r0und the c0rner.” Came a strange monotone, its robotic voice slightly muffled by the metal feet against the floor. “@rm y0ur$e|ves!”

She pressed the button hard.

“What is going on down there!?” Captain Strappi -fifth infantry- shrieked, tearing handfuls of wiry hair from his already balding, psoriasis infested scalp. “Give me a damage report!”

The Surveillance Quarters of Musket Station were still shaking from the recent explosion, and the captain was certainly not singing songs with a jolly grin and a mug full of some foul liquid. He was steaming, but not like how a kettle will scream at you to turn off a stove. He was the glowing-hot sword right out of the forge, dipped in a bucket of cool water.

He wasn’t in a good mood at all.

“Are any of you imbeciles hearin’ me!?”

“Sir, status report is as following, sir!” A willowy, clean-shaven little man with long legs, long arms, and a little body bounced madly from his chair, preforming a wild salute.

The floor gave another shudder and he was forced out of balance and tossed into his computer screen. The man closest to him, his body resembling a coffin with a tomato as a head, quickly took up the duty of reciting what was wrong.

“The 57th Chute was been completely and utterly destroyed, sir. An untraced explosion made a gaping hole in the wall, sir, and the suction rate has been, so far, quite dramatic. We’ll need to enter an atmosphere, and quick, if we are to repair the damage done, sir.”

The willowy watcher raised a hand weakly. “What he said.”

“WHAT!?” Strappi roared, pulling frantically at one of his ears. “We can’t make a bleedin’ emergency landing now! Everyone’s going to suspect an attack!”

“Sir, the explosion was well placed, sir.” Another watcher called. “It made no sound and no ball of flame- some sort of technology I’ve never seen before, sir. But, aside with this confusion, we know that it took out Chutes 56 and 58, as well as the 12th Bathing Rooms and a couple bedrooms, sir.”

"WHA-!?"

She snuck across the second tier of the Musket’s Boarding Room, unseen to anyone below. After a minute, she unslung a long, black case from her shoulder and set it lightly on the carpeted ground, flipping open the clasps with a single, graceful movement and sliding open the lid.

“I expect to get paid. I hope you understand that, you swine, and I hope, for your sake, that you honor your end of the agreement.”

A man in a striped tuxedo, shades, a shaved face, and a black, oiled widow’s peak grinned widely. His teeth were unnaturally white, and, if you could see him beneath the darkened glasses, he had green eyes that resembled those of a scowling man wearing a smiling mask.

“And you will be compensated appropriately, I never said you wouldn’t.” He assured the man, giving a notable wink.

“Then where’s my money!?” The bounty hunter leaped to his feet, sending his chair spinning to the ground. He slammed two leather-gloved hands onto the table, which sent a shudder through the glass vase and bouquet of flowers on the center of the counter.

“You will not have time to buy anything before your next job. So I won’t weigh you down with gold.”

“Give me my reward or the deal’s over.”

“If you back out now, you’ll walk out the door in pieces.” The fashionable man’s wide grin twitched, as if strained. “But if you… dispose of your last target, you’ll walk out intact, with a dozen cases of jewels and coins.”

The bounty hunter sighed, kicking his chair back onto its four, delicately carved, oaken legs and slumping back into a defeated sitting position. “One last job. Then I’m out.”

The hired gun winced, but managed to keep the smile. “The Baron? You’re kidding, right?”

He had a job to do.

It was necessary.
