Part 6: The Crimson Spider

 

Fortunately, the girl- Rose, Daragota had called her- simply wanted to return to her village, meaning that I only had one passenger. My current bike was not the Rutcarver MKII/Valiance, but a much less impressive Umbraros Industries ‘Firespout’ motorbike. As such, any more than a single passenger had a tendency to drag it down.

 

Unfortunately, like Rover and the Frezija-clone/Zatorak before him, Daragota Ayzer spoke constantly.

 

“So,” he said, “’Rikeroné Gamen Vortex’. I think the Rikeroné at least is real- no offense, but it’s a bit of a mouthful for you to have chosen it yourself- unless your intent is to distract people by making them say it three times fast.”

 

Restraint failing, I rolled my eyes. “What about ‘Daragota’?”

 

My response only made him smirk. “Actually, where I come from, it means ‘Questioner’. I’ve always kind of liked that.”

 

“I see,” I growled, realizing that two years of near-solitude probably hadn’t improved my patience, “It certainly suits you. You haven’t stopped questioning for an entire hour.”

 

Deciding to ignore my comment, he continued, “Now, ‘Vortex’ makes sense. It’s a pretty common word. But where did the ‘Gamen’ come from?”

 

 “It was my grandmother’s name,” I answered, and this was true. Ryous’ wife and my grandmother had indeed been called Gamen Vortex.

 

“But,” I continued, stopping my bike so that I could look him in the eye, “Why are so convinced that it isn’t my name?”

 

He shrugged. “Two reasons. One, I’ve never heard of you before. Two, I’ve only known you for an hour, but already I can tell that it wouldn’t be your style. And I can tell you’re all about style. About making a performance.”

My glare was even sharper than before when I looked back at him. “You think you know me, Daragota? Others been closer than you are. But they were all wrong.”

 

Only after the words came out did I realize that I had started yelling. Not a good sign in terms of stealth. I might as well light up a beacon.

 

“Whoa, calm down,” Daragota said, “I didn’t realize this was such a sensitive subject.” 

 

“Look, all I’m saying is-” I began, but stopped suddenly with my mouth wide open. We had run into a pile of smoldering debris in front of us.

 

“What happened here?” Daragota asked, either to me or to no one in particular, as I scanned the twisted wreckage.

 

Something caught my eye; a scrap of paper, blown by the wind. When I saw the emblem stylized as a spider, red, even, I didn’t know whether to stare in shock or growl in disgust.

 

“This was meant for me,” I said, as much to myself as to Daragota, “This wasn’t an accident, this was a message- and it was directed at me.”

 

“How do you-”  Daragota began to ask, but stopped when he saw the paper.

 

The crimson spider. During the Ultimate Race, it had seemed like a harmless way to overcome my arachnophobia; by turning it into my symbol. But now someone else had taken the crimson spider as their own emblem. The nerve! They wanted me to find this, to know that they had- that they thought they had power over me!

“Family trouble? This seems pretty extreme for that.”

 

“No,” I replied, “I don’t have a sister. However sent this is trying to mess with my mind. Did you see the spider on the back? I once led a team called the Crimson Spiders. They used by own symbol to grab my attention, and then sent a barrage of meaningless but sinister game metaphors. This is the work of a madman, and when I find them, I am going to take this letter and shove it down their throat.”

 

Daragota winced. “Isn’t that a bit harsh?”

I donned my helmet once again. “I’m a harsh girl. It’s one of the first things you have to learn about me.” 

 

As soon as my HUD restarted, it pinpointed something I hadn’t noticed. Someone was standing at above me at the top of the cliff, watching me. The figure was cloaked in black, causing me to click my tongue in irritation. It was so obvious it was sickening.

 

“She wants to be found.” I said.

 

“Who?”

 

“My mystery ‘sister’,” I clarified, “…I guess.”

 

The figure extended a hand out of her cloak and went as far as to wave at me, then began to depart.

 

“Stay here.” I told Daragota as I mounted my bike.

 

Without further ado, I gave chase.

Part 7: The Riding Storm

 

At first, I was confident that I could catch up with her. She was on foot, and while the Firespout was no MKII, it was still a motorcycle. The extra speed should more than compensate for having to ride up the mountain trail to get to her.

 

At least, that’s what I thought. When my quarry took a running start and started flying, I knew I was in trouble. Rocket-powered jet boots. Now how did she get rocket-powered jet boots?

 

I could still catch up to her, of course. It would just take a bit more elbow grease to do it. Now, the Firespout didn’t have anything especially exciting- no flight modes or dual bladed machine guns- meaning that I’d have to chase her down the old-fashioned way.

 

Well, I thought to myself while reaching behind me for the grappling cable I kept in a compartment under the seat, almost the old-fashioned way.

 

Fortunately, it only took me a few seconds to grab the cable and return to my seat. If it had been any longer, my tires would have been punctured by the icicle spike sticking out of the road.

 

Ice? In the summer? In Valia?

 

No sooner had I steered away from the impending tire opener than I saw a ball of the stuff flying through the air. I had a moment to gauge my priorities; my first was to avoid the hurtling hailstone, and my second to keep my quarry from escaping. Asking why a chunk of ice had started flying towards me came in third.

 

There was no time for anything fancy…or was there?

 

I began to swing the grappling hook above my head, increasing its speed with each revolution. When its swinging speed reached its maximum, I flung it at the impending obstacle.

The hook latched on and buried itself in the chunk of ice, binding it to me through the rope. I drifted the Firespout into a turn, stopping it when I was perpendicular to my previous position. The hailstone missed me and kept flying, only to be caught by the rope. I immediately pulled the rope while the hailstone was still moving, redirecting it and letting go of the rope when it had revolved in a half circle.

 

Without my hands holding the rope, the giant hailstone flew like a pebble in a slingshot, launching in the direction I had been driving- right at my mysterious quarry. The improvised missile flew straight and true, only avoided by the cloaked figure by a last-second maneuver, and even then it glanced off her rocket-powered jet boots, sending her spinning.

 

The impact had cut the power to one of the boots, and with only one, I found, as did she, that stabilized flight was nearly impossible. The result; she came spiraling down.

 

It only took me a few moments to rush to the spot where she had landed. As a precaution, I had already drawn one of my admittedly aged but dependable flareguns. I was well aware that they were becoming obsolete weapons, something that was not helped by the considerable wear they’d received in the years I’d used them. I knew it was only a matter of days before I’d have to replace their power pack again.

 

“First Daragota, now you,” I said to my now-grounded foe, “It seems like the whole world is conspiring to make me late.”

 

She didn’t answer; instead, she raised a hand with the palm facing me. I hesitated and held my flaregun back, wondering what she could mean by signaling for me to stop.

I had my answer the moment afterwards. I was just barely able to duck to the side in time to avoid the- the bolt of ice- no, not of ice, but of sheer cold- that had materialized in her open palm and been sent hurtling towards me. As it was, it clipped my shoulder, which suddenly felt as though I had plunged it into arctic waters.

 

The ice had come from her!

 

I wondered to myself exactly how I had been able to avoid the bolt at all. My reflexes had always been a bit of a gift; I’ve always been able to move a bit faster than everybody else.

 

Maybe that’s why I’ve always had trouble being patient with everybody else.

 

But what I’d been doing ever since the Ultimate Race… the stunts I’d pulled… I’d been doing the impossible for years.

 

I shook those thoughts from my hand, deeming that they had no purpose at the current time. Jumping at her, I launched a midair spinning kick.

 

It was really just supposed to scare her. I hadn’t expected to knock her down, throwing off her hood and knocking off her mask.

 

We landed with her facing away from me, so I couldn’t actually see her face. But I could see her brilliant, sapphire tresses unfold.

 

There was only one person I had ever heard of, in my entire life, who has blue hair.

 

 “No…Frezija?!?”

 

“Hey,” my protégé said, coughing and turning to face me, “Long time no see.”
