Chapter Two – Part Eight

 

He was an Arnkada. Perhaps one of the most feared creatures anyone has ever known, even more so than the witches that controlled them. They had a wicked ability for trapping enemies and smashing them quickly; stories were told that it was always effortless.

 

They had a keen intelligence, allowing them to calculate things in seconds that would take a man hours. They could also hear quite well with their long, pointy ears, but it wasn’t their hearing that made men wary, it was their eyes.

 

As Rory peered in, he couldn’t help notice that his dark ruby red eyes were piercing through him with such intensity, he wanted to flee. The Arnkada had a tip of a smile appearing on his thin lips, as if he knew what the boy was thinking.

 

Rory stood and watched him for a minute, wondering how in the world a bunch of untrained villagers were able to catch such an evil, malevolent creature. Arnkada’s were so smart; he had to have let himself be captured, but why?

 

The creature scowled at him, and waited to see what he would do. Rory wanted to disappear out of the room, but he knew the Arnkada would laugh at him if he did so. Instead, he forced himself to move forward away from his cell, still disturbed by his deep red eyes.

 

He quickly glanced over each cell, more and more disappointed as he realized the rest were only men, calling, more like whimpering, for him to unlock them from their prison. He ignored them, but as he reached the end of the hall, his heart sank.

 

As he peered into one of the last two cells, he could feel his pulse pounding in his head. Still tied up, but in a sitting position, the goblin had his head lowered, grimly facing the ground. Just the sight of the creature made Rory’s face burn with anger, but he remained calm and watched for a minute.

Chapter Two – Part Nine 

 

Finally, the goblin looked up at the boy with a scowl. “What?”

 

Rory numbly thought of a billion things to say, dismayed that he had marched right in without a plan. Then, without allowing himself a second thought to take it back, he blurted out, “Where are those witches?” His voice echoed the old man’s words down to the exact tone.

 

The goblin briefly blinked in confusion, then opened his mouth to speak. “Ah. Am I going to be constantly pestered by questions I cannot hope to answer? If so, I think I’m going to take a nap instead,” He stated curtly.

 

Rory shuffled a small step back. “Can’t hope to answer? What do you mean?!” He banged his fist on the metal bars, creating a long, drawn-out low pitched knock.

 

“I mean I don’t know the answer.”

 

Rory frowned at the simplicity of the answer, feeling that this guy was useless. “But you work for them…You’ve got to know.” He had clung his hopes on this one creature, and here he was, dumbfounded. He would demand an answer from the Arnkada, but, even if he knew, he wouldn’t give an answer. He refused to admit that the beast scared him.

 

“I don’t work from them. Everyone always get it wrong. Just because we’re not human,” He almost snarled the word, “doesn’t mean we work for the witches. Pretty soon you people will be smashing your own steeds because they stepped right instead of left. I’m thrown in here for your own people’s ignorance. I didn’t do anything.”

Chapter Two – Part Two of Part Nine

 

Rory was stunned. He had never really thought about it that way, but then again, these were creatures with twisted minds and easily lying tongues. Without consciously thinking to do so, he glowered at the thought.

 

“Then why were you lurking in town?”

 

“Lurking? Why I think I was hunting in the forest and they snatched me up. Took my bow too.” The goblin then stared at Rory, wondering if he might have some kind of reaction, but the boy stood there, quieted.

 

“Do you have a name?”

 

“You act as if my kind doesn’t.”

 

“Alright, then what is it?” Rory asked, still standing outside the cold, dark cell.

 

“Arrol,” the goblin said, waiting for the boy to reply in kind. He paused a moment more then, “And yours?”

 

“Rory.”

 

A moment of silence grew between the two of them, until Arrol finally spoke. “What now? I have nothing for you.”

 

“You don’t belong in here. And I’m going to get you out.” A sudden rush struck Rory and somehow, somewhere, he couldn’t be sure where, he knew that the goblin had said the truth. He had committed nothing, and…did that mean he just didn’t fight when they captured him? He gave in. An attempt to not harm his capturers?

Chapter Two – Part Ten

 

Arrol stared at Rory for a minute, inspecting his face to see if he was jesting or not. When the boy’s face remained as serious as stone, he proceeded carefully. “Why, what have I done for you?”

 

Rory hastily answered, saying that it was wrongful for him to be in here. He didn’t provide details, since he was right and he didn’t know why, but it just felt like he should. “I’m not sure how I’m going to do it, though.”

 

“If I had that oversized knife of yours, then this would be child's play,” The goblin said, wriggling in his ropes.

 

 “Oh.” Rory reluctantly slid his sword from his belt, and stared at it for a quick second, pondering how quickly he could move incase Arrol turned ugly and decided to use the weapon against him. Still, he reluctantly shoved it inside the cell, and cautiously took a few stumbling steps back.

 

Arrol grabbed at the sword with his feet, and snatched it up, maneuvering his legs backwards through the ropes. The blade nicked his legs, but he paid it no mind and flung the weapon towards his chest. For a second, Rory thought the blade was going to kill him, but it landed harmlessly in his lap to where his hands were. He squirmed in a strange fashion, contorting his lean figure. As if he were an expert, he set the dull edge to saw against the ropes that bound him. He used his knees to hold the handle, and within seconds, he was rolling his wrists around in circles, free of restraints.

 

With a single defiant slice, he cut the ropes around his legs, and stood up.

 

Rory’s eyes bulged big and wide, or at least he thought they did. He had to be at least seven feet tall? Maybe more? His judgment of length was skewed considering the dim light, but he had seemed of normal height when he was sitting scrunched up. Maybe even short.
