@ Jonna: Well, the way I personally see it is that everyone is destined for greatness and to be an awesome good guy (or good girl), but it's up to a person (who has the freedom to choose) wether or not they fulfill their destiny, which makes it all the more sad when they use their lives for evil, because they were destined for so much more. However, though, in fictious universes that have time travel (such as the universe of Doctor Who) when you travel in time, there are some things in history that you can change (such as saving the life of a random person you know nothing about, but you can't use time-travel as an undo button, like for instance to stop a loved one from getting into an accident and kicking the bucket, because then in the new timeline you would have created, your loved one would've just been around the whole time, and you would've never gotten the notion to travel back in time to save them in the first place, so when the time came that you would've travelled back in time to save him or her, you wouldn't do it, and the world would back to the timeline when they were gone, and so you would have the notion to travel back in time and save your loved one, and then when the time came, you would, and it would revert back to the timeline when your loved one was alive, and so-on and so-forth never ending. This is known as a time paradox, and I believe The Doctor said it could destroy reality, or something like that. Also in the "Whoniverse", if you will: most of time is in flux, allowing for history to be altered by time-travel, or saved by it. However, there are moments known as "fixed points in time" which are immensely significant moments in history, which, as of such, cannot be altered by traveling back in time whatsoever, now matter how amazingly wonderful or horrifically awful they were, such as WWII, the Moon Landing, or Pompeii.
